Surah Al-Ma'arij

The Ascending Stairways • Makkah • 44 Verses
A profound Makkan Surah detailing the relativity of Divine time, the terrifying selfishness of the human ego on the Day of Judgment, and the specific psychological anxieties that plague humanity in the worldly life.
Verse 1
سَأَلَ سَآئِلٌۢ بِعَذَابٍۢ وَاقِعٍۢ
"A supplicant asked for a punishment bound to happen"
Plain Understanding
The Surah opens with a shocking report. Instead of asking God for guidance, mercy, or clarity, a specific individual arrogantly and sarcastically prayed for God's severe punishment to strike him immediately. God confirms that the punishment he requested is indeed guaranteed to happen.
Historical Context
Classical exegetes, including Ibn Abbas, identify this "supplicant" as An-Nadr ibn al-Harith (or Abu Jahl). Frustrated by the Prophet's ﷺ warnings, he openly stood before the Kaaba and issued a mocking challenge recorded elsewhere in the Qur'an (Anfal: 32): "O Allah, if this is indeed the truth from You, then rain down upon us stones from the sky or bring us a painful punishment!" He was subsequently killed at the Battle of Badr.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Imam Al-Qurtubi highlights the profound psychological sickness of the Makkan elite. Their hatred for the Prophet ﷺ was so intense that they preferred total physical annihilation over the humiliation of admitting they were wrong and submitting to the truth. Arrogance blinds the intellect to the point of self-destruction.

Verse 3
مِّنَ ٱللَّهِ ذِى ٱلْمَعَارِجِ
"[It is] from Allah, owner of the ways of ascent."
Plain Understanding
The punishment originates directly from Allah, who introduces Himself with a unique title: "Dhi Al-Ma'arij" (The Owner of the Ascending Stairways/Pathways). This refers to the massive, structured spiritual and physical pathways through which angels, decrees, and human deeds ascend to the heavens.
Purification of the Self (Al-Gilani)
Sheikh Abdul Qadir al-Gilani interprets the "Ma'arij" on a spiritual level. The journey to the Divine Presence is not a horizontal walk; it is a vertical, strenuous climb. To ascend these spiritual stairways, the believer must continuously drop the heavy weights of worldly attachments and ego at each progressive step. You cannot ascend while carrying the Dunya.

Verse 4
تَعْرُجُ ٱلْمَلَـٰٓئِكَةُ وَٱلرُّوحُ إِلَيْهِ فِى يَوْمٍۢ كَانَ مِقْدَارُهُۥ خَمْسِينَ أَلْفَ سَنَةٍۢ
"The angels and the Spirit will ascend to Him during a Day the extent of which is fifty thousand years."
Plain Understanding
God defines the relativistic scale of divine time and space. The angels, led by the "Spirit" (the Archangel Gabriel), traverse these cosmic stairways. The Day of Judgment is described as having a duration equivalent to 50,000 earthly years, emphasizing the agonizing, seemingly endless waiting period the disbelievers will endure while standing for judgment.
Historical Context
When this verse was revealed, the companions were terrified by the sheer length of a 50,000-year day standing under the sun. The Prophet ﷺ comforted them, stating: "By Him in whose hand is my soul, it will be lightened for the believer until it becomes lighter than the time it takes to perform an obligatory prayer in the worldly life." (Musnad Ahmad). Time on that Day is subjective and entirely dependent on one's spiritual state.

Verse 5
فَٱصْبِرْ صَبْرًۭا جَمِيلًا
"So be patient with gracious patience."
Plain Understanding
In response to the arrogant mockeries of the elite and the terrifying reality of the impending Judgment, God commands the Prophet ﷺ to adopt "Sabr Jameel" (Beautiful, Gracious Patience). He is instructed not to rush their punishment or act out of frustration.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali defines "Sabr Jameel." Standard patience is enduring a hardship while complaining about it to others. "Beautiful Patience" is swallowing the bitter pill of hardship with such grace and dignity that people looking at you think you are in a state of absolute blessing. You complain of your grief to God alone.

Verse 10
وَلَا يَسْـَٔلُ حَمِيمٌ حَمِيمًۭا
"And no devoted friend will ask [anything of] a devoted friend,"
Plain Understanding
The sheer terror of this cosmic collapse will entirely erase all human bonds. A "Hameem" is a fiercely loyal, intimate friend who would normally sacrifice everything to help you in the worldly life. On this Day, they will walk right past each other, too paralyzed by their own terror to even ask how the other is doing.

Imam al-Ghazali issues a profound warning. In the Dunya, people constantly compromise their religion, lie, and commit sins to please their friends, bosses, or social circles. Yet, on the Day of Judgment, those exact same friends will completely ignore them. Do not sacrifice the Eternal Friend (God) for temporary friends who will abandon you when you need them most.

Verse 11

Yet, they intentionally look away. The terror of Hell is so absolute that a guilty person would gladly take their own beloved children and throw them into the Fire if it meant buying their own escape.

It loved its children in the Dunya only for the emotional comfort and legacy they provided, not out of true, selfless love. In the Akhirah, the mask falls off.

Verse 18
وَجَمَعَ فَأَوْعَىٰ
"And collected [wealth] and hoarded."
Plain Understanding
The second specific crime that guarantees entry into this Fire is ethical and financial. Alongside their rejection of the truth, this person obsessively gathered wealth, locked it securely away in a container or vault ("Aw'a"), and selfishly refused to spend it on the poor, effectively hoarding the provisions God intended for society.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali states that hoarding wealth is a physical manifestation of a severe theological failure: the lack of trust in God (Tawakkul). The one who hoards secretly believes that their vault of money, rather than the Creator, is what sustains their life. The fire explicitly punishes this subtle form of idolatry.

Verse 19
إِنَّ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنَ خُلِقَ هَلُوعًا
"Indeed, mankind was created anxious:"
Plain Understanding
God provides a direct psychological diagnosis of the human condition. The default, biological state of the human ego is "Halu'a"—a profound, inherent sense of fretfulness, instability, and chronic anxiety.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Imam Al-Qurtubi explains that the term "Halu'a" cannot be translated with a single word. It is a specific psychological complex combining two extreme reactions: absolute panic during times of hardship, and absolute stinginess during times of ease. God embedded this vulnerability in human nature so that man could never find absolute independence or peace within himself, forcing him to seek the Creator.
Purification of the Self (Ibn Ata'illah)
Ibn Ata'illah notes the mercy in this design: "If He had created you completely self-sufficient and at peace in the Dunya, you would have forgotten Him. The anxiety woven into your chest is the very rope God uses to pull you back to His door."

Verse 20
إِذَا مَسَّهُ ٱلشَّرُّ جَزُوعًۭا
"When evil touches him, impatient,"
Plain Understanding
The first symptom of this inherent anxiety is described. When a human being is merely "touched" by hardship (such as illness, poverty, or loss), they immediately fall into "Jazu'a"—a state of paralyzing despair, loud complaining, and a complete loss of patience and perspective.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali highlights the ego's dramatic lack of vision. The verse specifically says when evil *touches* him. A mere touch of discomfort causes the unpurified ego to act as if the entire world is ending. It completely forgets the decades of health and provision it enjoyed prior to that single touch of hardship.

Verse 21
وَإِذَا مَسَّهُ ٱلْخَيْرُ مَنُوعًا
"And when good touches him, withholding [of it],"
Plain Understanding
The second symptom of this anxiety is greed. When the human is touched by good (wealth, health, power, or success), they become "Manu'a"—intensely protective, hoarding the blessing, and fiercely withholding it from others out of a deep-seated fear of losing it.
Purification of the Self (Al-Gilani)
Sheikh Abdul Qadir al-Gilani points out the spiritual contradiction. Stinginess is the physical manifestation of a lack of *Tawakkul* (Trust in God). The person hoards the wealth because their heart secretly believes that their bank account, not the Creator, is their ultimate sustainer. They withhold out of a terrifying illusion of scarcity.

Verse 23

"Those who are constant in their prayer,"
Plain Understanding
God qualifies what type of prayer cures anxiety. It is not the occasional, desperate prayer of someone in trouble, but a prayer that is "Da'imun" (constant, continuous, and firmly established as a permanent habit regardless of life's circumstances).
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali explains that spiritual medicine is like physical medicine; it only cures the disease if taken on a strict, continuous schedule. Dropping the prayer when times are good and picking it up when times are bad is a symptom of the ego, not the behavior of a true servant. Constancy (*Mudawamah*) builds the fortress that protects the heart from panic.

Verse 24

"And those within whose wealth is a known right"
Plain Understanding
To cure the specific symptom of greed, the believer must adopt a radical shift in their perspective on ownership. They recognize that a specifically calculated percentage of their wealth (Zakah and obligatory charity) is a "known right" that literally does not belong to them.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Al-Qurtubi points out the profound psychological framing of the phrase "a known right." When you give charity, you are not doing the poor person a favor; you are merely handing over what God already designated as *their* property. Recognizing this completely annihilates the ego's arrogance when giving wealth.

Verse 25
لِّلسَّآئِلِ وَٱلْمَحْرُومِ
"For the petitioner and the deprived -"
Plain Understanding
This known right is distributed to two categories of people: the "Sa'il" (the one who actively asks or begs for help because of their desperate need) and the "Mahrum" (the deprived person who is deeply impoverished but maintains such personal dignity that they refuse to ask others for help).
Historical Context
The Prophet ﷺ actively trained his companions to hunt for the "Mahrum." He stated in an authentic Hadith: "The poor person is not the one who goes round the people and is dismissed with one or two morsels. The poor person is the one who does not have enough to satisfy him, but he is not recognized so that alms may be given to him, and he does not stand up to beg." (Sahih Bukhari). This verse commands the believer to actively investigate their community to find those silently suffering.

"And those who believe in the Day of Recompense"
Plain Understanding

Ibn Ata'illah states that true *Tasdeeq* (confirmation) is not proven by the tongue; it is proven by behavior. If you truly believed that tomorrow your entire life's record would be scrutinized by the King, your actions today would radically shift. You only disobey God to the degree that your certainty in the Day of Recompense wavers.

Verse 27

"And those who are fearful of the punishment of their Lord -"
Plain Understanding
Despite their constant prayers, their charity, and their firm faith, they do not become arrogant or complacent. They maintain "Shafaqah" (a cautious, tender, and protective fear) regarding God's judgment, constantly worried that their deeds might not be sincere enough to be accepted.

Imam al-Ghazali explains that *Shafaqah* is not a paralyzing terror that drives a person away from God; it is a profound reverence combined with love—like a mother's intense fear when her child walks too close to a fire. It is a protective fear that actively drives the believer closer to God's sanctuary.

Verse 28

"Indeed, the punishment of their Lord is not that from which one is safe -"
Plain Understanding
God confirms the validity of their fear. No human being, regardless of their piety, knowledge, or status, is guaranteed absolute immunity or safety from God's justice until they have successfully crossed the bridge over Hellfire in the Hereafter.

This reality was deeply internalized by the greatest companions. Umar ibn al-Khattab famously stated: "If a voice from heaven announced that all of mankind would enter Paradise except for one man, I would fear that I am that man." The absolute greatest generations never felt "safe" (Ma'mun) from their own potential to slip. Feeling guaranteed Paradise is the ultimate trap of arrogance.

Verse 32

"And those who are to their trusts and promises attentive"
Plain Understanding
The profile shifts to societal integrity. The successful believer is fiercely protective of "Amanah" (anything entrusted to them, whether it is physical property, a secret, or a leadership position) and they never break their "Ahd" (contracts and promises).

He explicitly linked keeping trusts to the core of faith, stating: "The signs of a hypocrite are three: whenever he speaks, he tells a lie; whenever he promises, he always breaks it; and whenever he is trusted, he betrays that trust." (Sahih Bukhari). True faith is impossible without societal reliability.

Verse 34

"And those who [carefully] maintain their prayer:"
Plain Understanding
The extensive list of traits circles perfectly back to where it began: the prayer. But here, the focus shifts. They don't just pray constantly; they strictly "maintain" and guard (Yuhafidhun) their prayers, ensuring they are performed at the correct times, with the correct physical conditions, and with deep internal focus (Khushu').

Al-Qurtubi points out the brilliant symmetry. The description of the successful believer begins in Verse 23 with *Da'imun* (consistency in the act) and ends here in Verse 34 with *Yuhafidhun* (guarding its quality and conditions). True spiritual success is the fusion of both volume (showing up every time) and quality (being deeply present).

Verse 35
أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ فِى جَنَّـٰتٍۢ مُّكْرَمُونَ
"Those will be in gardens, honored."
Plain Understanding
God states the ultimate, guaranteed result for those who construct their character using this divine formula. They will be granted eternal Gardens of Paradise, and more importantly, they will be given the status of "Mukramun" (highly honored and deeply respected by the Creator and the angels).
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
Ibn Ata'illah draws the final distinction: the reward is not just the physical Garden, but the spiritual reality of being "Honored" (Mukram). To sit in a beautiful garden is a bodily pleasure; to be personally honored and welcomed by the King of Kings is the ultimate pleasure of the soul, far transcending the physical delights of Paradise itself.
