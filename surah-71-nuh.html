Surah Nuh

Noah • Makkah • 28 Verses
A profound historical document detailing the 950-year mission of Prophet Noah. It serves as a masterclass in the methodology of calling people to God, the psychological resistance of the arrogant, and the direct link between seeking forgiveness and receiving earthly provision.
Verse 1
إِنَّآ أَرْسَلْنَا نُوحًا إِلَىٰ قَوْمِهِۦٓ أَنْ أَنذِرْ قَوْمَكَ مِن قَبْلِ أَن يَأْتِيَهُمْ عَذَابٌ أَلِيمٌۭ
"Indeed, We sent Noah to his people, [saying], 'Warn your people before there comes to them a painful punishment.'"
Plain Understanding
God initiates the narrative by confirming Noah's (Nuh's) divine commission. The overarching objective of his massive mission is established immediately: he must act as a clear, urgent alarm system for his society, giving them the opportunity to change course before a devastating Divine punishment strikes them.
Historical Context
This Surah was revealed during a period of extreme frustration and persecution in Mecca. The Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) was facing a wall of stubborn rejection from the Quraysh. God revealed the entire struggle of Noah as a direct mirror and profound comfort to the Prophet ﷺ, proving that immense societal resistance is the historical standard for all Messengers.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Imam Al-Qurtubi notes that the sequence of sending a warner *before* the punishment arrives highlights God's absolute, flawless justice. The Creator never enacts destruction upon a civilization without first establishing the intellectual and spiritual proof against them through a Messenger.

Verse 2
قَالَ يَـٰقَوْمِ إِنِّى لَكُمْ نَذِيرٌۭ مُّبِينٌ
"He said, 'O my people, indeed I am to you a clear warner,'"
Plain Understanding
Noah immediately executes his mission. He approaches his society using a tender, relational plea ("O my people") to emphasize that he is one of them and desires their survival. He declares his role without ambiguity: he is a clear, transparent warner with no hidden political agenda.
Purification of the Self (Al-Gilani)
Sheikh Abdul Qadir al-Gilani reflects on the etiquette of the spiritual guide (Murshid). The true caller to God does not seek to dominate, embarrass, or crush the sinners. By saying "O my people," Noah speaks from a place of deep compassion, viewing the disbelievers not as enemies to be destroyed, but as sick family members desperately needing a cure.

Verse 3
أَنِ ٱعْبُدُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَٱتَّقُوهُ وَأَطِيعُونِ
"'[Saying], Worship Allah, fear Him and obey me.'"
Plain Understanding
Noah distills his entire 950-year message into three highly actionable pillars: 1) Dedicate all worship exclusively to God (Tawhid). 2) Maintain a protective fear and reverence for His boundaries (Taqwa). 3) Follow the practical, lived leadership of the Messenger (Obedience).
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali breaks down this tri-fold formula. "Worship Allah" secures the internal intention of the heart. "Fear Him" ensures the abstention from sins and forbidden acts. "Obey me" dictates the external execution of righteous deeds according to the prophetic model. Together, these three commands encompass the absolute entirety of religion.

Verse 4
يَغْفِرْ لَكُم مِّن ذُنُوبِكُمْ وَيُؤَخِّرْكُمْ إِلَىٰٓ أَجَلٍۢ مُّسَمًّى ۚ إِنَّ أَجَلَ ٱللَّهِ إِذَا جَآءَ لَا يُؤَخَّرُ ۖ لَوْ كُنتُمْ تَعْلَمُونَ
"'Allah will forgive you of your sins and delay you for a specified term. Indeed, the time [set by] Allah, when it comes, cannot be delayed, if you only knew.'"
Plain Understanding
Noah offers an incredible incentive for their submission. If they implement the three pillars, God will completely erase their past crimes and cancel the decree of their premature destruction, allowing them to live out their full, natural lifespans in peace. However, he warns that once God's ultimate, unchangeable deadline arrives, no negotiations or delays will be accepted.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Al-Qurtubi extracts a profound legal and theological reality from this verse: Righteousness and obedience can actually extend a society's lifespan by removing the decree of divine punishment, while widespread sin literally cuts a civilization's lifespan short. The ultimate "specified term" (Ajal) of death is fixed, but the path to reaching it is affected by human morality.

Verse 5
قَالَ رَبِّ إِنِّى دَعَوْتُ قَوْمِى لَيْلًۭا وَنَهَارًۭا
"He said, 'My Lord, indeed I invited my people [to truth] night and day.'"
Plain Understanding
The narrative flashes forward centuries later. Noah is presenting his formal, exhausted report to his Creator. He confirms that he did not preach casually or occasionally; he dedicated absolutely every waking hour, sacrificing his rest, to call them to salvation throughout the darkness of night and the light of day.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
Ibn Ata'illah states that true servitude ('Ubudiyyah) is not defined by the success of the outcome, but by the relentless consistency of the effort. Noah is praised eternally not because his people converted (they didn't), but because he never stopped calling them, proving his absolute, unshakeable loyalty to the Divine Command regardless of the results.

Verse 6

"'But my invitation increased them not except in flight.'"
Plain Understanding
Noah reports the devastating paradox of his mission. Despite his deep sincerity and exhaustion, his efforts produced the exact opposite of the desired result. The more he urged them toward God's forgiveness and salvation, the faster and further they ran away from the truth.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali explains the psychology of this "flight." When a heart is completely colonized by arrogance and worldly desires, it becomes diseased. Just as a person with a severe eye infection feels intense physical pain when exposed to bright sunlight, the diseased ego feels severe psychological pain when exposed to the pure light of revelation, causing it to flee into the darkness to protect its illness.

Verse 7
وَإِنِّى كُلَّمَا دَعَوْتُهُمْ لِتَغْفِرَ لَهُمْ جَعَلُوٓا۟ أَصَـٰبِعَهُمْ فِىٓ ءَاذَانِهِمْ وَٱسْتَغْشَوْا۟ ثِيَابَهُمْ وَأَصَرُّوا۟ وَٱسْتَكْبَرُوا۟ ٱسْتِكْبَارًۭا
"'And indeed, every time I invited them that You may forgive them, they put their fingers in their ears, covered themselves with their garments, persisted, and were arrogant with great arrogance.'"
Plain Understanding
Noah vividly details the intensely childish, hostile, and physical reaction of his society. Whenever he approached them with the offer of God's forgiveness, they physically shoved their fingers into their ears to block out his voice, pulled their heavy cloaks over their heads so they wouldn't even have to look at him, and doubled down on their massive, stubborn pride.
Historical Context
This imagery directly paralleled the exact hostility Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) faced. The elite of Quraysh would intentionally make loud noises, whistle, cover their faces, and run away when the Prophet recited the Qur'an near the Kaaba, desperately trying to prevent the divine words from penetrating their hearts or the hearts of the pilgrims. God is showing the Prophet ﷺ that the blueprint of arrogance has not changed in thousands of years.

Verse 9
ثُمَّ إِنِّىٓ أَعْلَنتُ لَهُمْ وَأَسْرَرْتُ لَهُمْ إِسْرَارًۭا
"'Then I announced to them and [also] confided to them secretly'"
Plain Understanding
He further diversified his methodology. He held massive public rallies ("A'lantu"), and he simultaneously visited individuals in the absolute privacy of their own homes ("Asrartu"), tailoring his approach to fit every possible psychological, social, and political dynamic in a desperate bid to save them.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Al-Qurtubi extracts the fundamental methodology of Islamic outreach (Da'wah) from this verse. A true caller to God cannot rely on a single method. They must be dynamic—utilizing public platforms to establish general truth and societal norms, while employing private, gentle, and confidential counsel to address personal doubts and specific sins, leaving no strategic stone unturned.

Verse 10
فَقُلْتُ ٱسْتَغْفِرُوا۟ رَبَّكُمْ إِنَّهُۥ كَانَ غَفَّارًۭا
"'And said, 'Ask forgiveness of your Lord. Indeed, He is ever a Perpetual Forgiver.'"
Plain Understanding
Through all of these diverse methods, Noah's core message remained a profound offer of mercy. He urged his people to seek "Istighfar" (active forgiveness). He assured them that God is not just a forgiver, but "Ghaffar"—One who perpetually, continuously, and unconditionally forgives, regardless of the magnitude, frequency, or severity of the sins committed.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
Ibn Ata'illah teaches that the primary weapon of the devil is convincing the sinner that their crimes are too massive for God to forgive, leading them into despair. Noah counters this satanic trap by emphasizing the Name "Ghaffar." Your sins are finite, restricted by time and space, but His forgiveness is infinite. Never weigh your finite crimes against His infinite mercy.

Verse 11
يُرْسِلِ ٱلسَّمَآءَ عَلَيْكُم مِّدْرَارًۭا
"'He will send [rain from] the sky upon you in [continuing] showers'"
Plain Understanding
Noah explicitly links spiritual purity to environmental and economic prosperity. If they humble themselves and seek forgiveness, God will end their severe droughts. He will open the skies and send down abundant, continuous, and highly beneficial rain ("Midrara") to revive their dead lands.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Al-Qurtubi records a famous historical incident regarding this verse. Whenever people came to the great scholar Hasan al-Basri complaining of severe drought, intense poverty, or an inability to have children, his answer to all of them was identical: "Seek forgiveness from Allah (Make Istighfar)." When asked why he gave the same spiritual cure for different physical problems, he simply recited this sequence of verses from Surah Nuh. Istighfar is the master key that unlocks physical sustenance.

Verse 16
وَجَعَلَ ٱلْقَمَرَ فِيهِنَّ نُورًۭا وَجَعَلَ ٱلشَّمْسَ سِرَاجًۭا
"And made the moon therein a [reflected] light and made the sun a burning lamp?"
Plain Understanding
Noah highlights the specific celestial bodies that regulate their daily lives. The Qur'an uses precise language here: the moon is described as "Noor" (a gentle, reflected light that illuminates the dark), while the sun is explicitly termed a "Siraj" (a blazing, self-generating lamp that produces intense heat and energy).
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali extracts a profound spiritual metaphor from this astronomy. The Sun (Siraj) represents the ultimate, burning truth and majesty of the Creator. The human eye cannot stare directly at the sun without going blind. The Moon (Noor) represents the Prophet; he absorbs the intense light of the Divine and reflects it gently into the darkness of society so the people can navigate without being blinded by the sheer intensity of the Divine Presence.

"And Allah has caused you to grow from the earth a [progressive] growth."

Noah brings the argument back down to earth with a beautifully humbling metaphor. He equates human existence to agriculture. Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
Ibn Ata'illah states: "Do not let the branches of your intellect reach arrogantly into the sky when you know that the roots of your existence are planted in the dirt." This verse violently strikes the ego of the arrogant elite; they are merely plants harvested from the mud.

Al-Qurtubi emphasizes that Noah uses the indisputable reality of death (returning to the earth) to prove the disputed reality of resurrection (extraction). Since they cannot deny the first half of the process, it is entirely irrational to deny the Creator's capability to execute the second half.

Verse 21

"Noah said, 'My Lord, indeed they have disobeyed me and followed him whose wealth and children will not increase him except in loss.'"
Plain Understanding
After nearly a millennium of presenting these flawless logical, biological, and cosmological proofs, Noah issues his final, exhausted report. The masses completely disobeyed him. They chose to follow the billionaires who signed their paychecks rather than the Prophet who offered them salvation.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
Ibn Ata'illah states that whenever wealth and power are decoupled from the fear of God, they cease to be blessings and immediately transform into instruments of "Khasar" (absolute loss). The elite thought their money bought them influence; in reality, it only bought them a deeper level of Hellfire.

Verse 23

"'And said, 'Never leave your gods and never leave Wadd or Suwa' or Yaghuth and Ya'uq and Nasr.''"
Plain Understanding
The core of their massive propaganda plan is revealed. The elite instructed the masses to be fiercely patriotic and aggressively defend their ancestral religion. They specifically commanded them to never abandon their five primary, named idols: Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr.
Historical Context
This verse contains a profound historical revelation regarding the origin of idolatry. Ibn Abbas narrated in Sahih Al-Bukhari: "These were the names of pious, righteous men from the people of Noah. When they died, Satan inspired their people to erect statues in the places where they used to sit, so they could remember their piety. They were not worshipped at first. But when that generation died and the true knowledge was lost, the later generations began worshipping the statues as actual gods." Tragically, centuries later, the Arab tribes of Quraysh, Kalb, and Hawazin adopted these exact same idols and worshipped them in Mecca.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali points out the sinister strategy of the Devil (Tadarruj). He rarely commands a person to commit massive sin (like Shirk) on day one. He starts by hijacking a good intention—"Let's honor these righteous men"—and slowly twists it over generations until it becomes the greatest crime in existence.

Verse 28 ۩
رَّبِّ ٱغْفِرْ لِى وَلِوَٰلِدَىَّ وَلِمَن دَخَلَ بَيْتِىَ مُؤْمِنًۭا وَلِلْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَٱلْمُؤْمِنَـٰتِ وَلَا تَزِدِ ٱلظَّـٰلِمِينَ إِلَّا تَبَارًۢا
"'My Lord, forgive me and my parents and whoever enters my house a believer and the believing men and believing women. And do not increase the wrongdoers except in destruction.'"
Plain Understanding
The Surah concludes with Noah's beautiful, comprehensive closing prayer. Before praying for the believers or against the oppressors, he humbly begs God to forgive his own shortcomings, then extends his prayer to his parents, his physical household, and finally to all believing men and women until the end of time. He finalizes the curse, asking God to guarantee the absolute destruction of the oppressors.
Purification of the Self (Al-Gilani)
Sheikh Abdul Qadir al-Gilani highlights the absolute, staggering humility of the Prophets. After 950 years of brutal, exhausting service, torture, and unwavering loyalty to God, Noah does not stand before God demanding a VIP reward. His very first words are: "My Lord, forgive me." He views his 950 years of flawless effort as inadequate, seeking only the mercy of his Master.
