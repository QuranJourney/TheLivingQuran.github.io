Surah At-Takwir

The Overthrowing • Makkah • 29 Verses
The Unveiling of Reality. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever wishes to look at the Day of Resurrection as if he were seeing it with his own eyes, let him read Surah At-Takwir." It details the collapse of the physical universe, leading to the ultimate exposure of the soul.
Verse 1
إِذَا ٱلشَّمْسُ كُوِّرَتْ
"When the sun is wrapped up [in darkness]"
Plain Understanding
The sequence of destruction begins with the ultimate source of earthly energy. The sun, which sustains all biology and vision, is "Kuwwirat" (overthrown, rolled up, and extinguished). Its massive, blazing light is entirely snuffed out, plunging the solar system into absolute, terrifying darkness.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Imam Al-Qurtubi explains the linguistic root of "Kuwwirat." It refers to the act of folding or wrapping something around itself, like wrapping a turban around a head. The sun—worshipped by millions throughout human history as a supreme, invincible deity—is casually folded up and discarded by its true Creator, utterly destroying the greatest false idol in the sky.

Verse 2
وَإِذَا ٱلنُّجُومُ ٱنكَدَرَتْ
"And when the stars fall, dispersing,"
Plain Understanding
The structural failure spreads to the deeper cosmos. The trillions of stars, which humans rely upon for navigation and astronomical calculations, lose their fixed orbits and their luminosity. They "Inkadarat"—dimming, scattering, and plummeting like dust into the cosmic void.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali points out the psychological strike of this verse. If the massive, ancient, burning stars that have outlived empires are easily extinguished and scattered like leaves in the wind, how can the fragile human ego possibly believe that its worldly legacy, bank account, or status will last? The illusion of permanence is shattered.

The catastrophe strikes the earth's surface. The mountains, representing the absolute pinnacle of earthly weight and unshakeable stability, are completely uprooted from their foundations and set into motion ("Suyyirat"), blowing away like weightless clouds of dust.

Verse 4
وَإِذَا ٱلْعِشَارُ عُطِّلَتْ
"And when full-term she-camels are neglected"
Plain Understanding
God pivots from cosmic destruction to human reaction. The "'Ishaar" refers specifically to she-camels that are exactly ten months pregnant. In ancient Arabia, this was the ultimate, most heavily guarded and cherished form of wealth (representing both current value and guaranteed future dividends). On this Day, they will be entirely neglected ("'Uttilat") and abandoned by their owners.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
Ibn Ata'illah extracts the universal lesson behind the camel. In every era, humanity has an "'Ishaar"—a luxury car, a stock portfolio, a massive estate. The verse proves that the terror of God's account is so paralyzing that the human being will instantly and voluntarily abandon the very wealth they spent their entire life desperately accumulating. At the moment of truth, materialism instantly evaporates.

In their absolute panic, the lion stands next to the gazelle, entirely forgetting its instinct to hunt, just as the gazelle forgets its instinct to flee. The fear of God overrides biology.

Verse 7
وَإِذَا ٱلنُّفُوسُ زُوِّجَتْ
"And when the souls are paired"
Plain Understanding
The administrative sorting of the Day of Judgment begins. The "pairing" here does not mean marriage. It means souls are strictly sorted, categorized, and grouped together based entirely on their spiritual states. The tyrant is paired with the tyrants, the hypocrite with the hypocrites, and the righteous with the righteous.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Al-Qurtubi highlights the terrifying social reality of this pairing. In the Dunya, a righteous person is often forced to live among corrupt people due to family or work, and a corrupt person hides among the righteous in the mosque. On the Day of Judgment, this mixing is terminated. You will be physically grouped exclusively with those whose internal desires matched your own.

Verse 8
وَإِذَا ٱلْمَوْءُۥدَةُ سُئِلَتْ
"And when the girl [who was] buried alive is asked"
Plain Understanding
God initiates the supreme court of justice with the most horrific, undeniable crime. The "Maw'oodah" refers to the infant girl who was buried alive by her own parents. On the Day of Judgment, this murdered infant is resurrected, brought to the center of the divine court, and formally interrogated.
Historical Context
In pre-Islamic Arabia (Jahiliyyah), certain tribes practiced female infanticide out of extreme poverty or out of a twisted, arrogant pride (fearing a daughter might eventually bring shame to the tribe). The infant was often buried alive in the desert sand. Islam violently eradicated this practice, elevating the status of daughters to a ticket into Paradise.

Verse 9
بِأَىِّ ذَنۢبٍۢ قُتِلَتْ
"For what sin she was killed"
Plain Understanding
God explicitly asks the murdered infant: "What exact crime did you commit to deserve such a brutal execution?" The question is not asked because God doesn't know the answer; it is asked to publicly and permanently humiliate, terrify, and convict the murderer.
Purification of the Self (Al-Gilani)
Sheikh Abdul Qadir al-Gilani notes the profound psychology of Divine Justice here. God entirely ignores the arrogant murderer in the beginning. By addressing the victim directly and asking her to testify, God restores the dignity she was stripped of in the dirt, while the tyrant is forced to stand silently in agonizing terror, waiting for the verdict he knows is coming.

Verse 14

"A soul will [then] know what it has brought [with it]."
Plain Understanding
This is the shattering conclusion to the massive twelve-part sequence. When the stars fall, the oceans burn, the murdered infant speaks, and Hell is ignited—in that exact moment, all earthly self-deception, excuses, and justifications instantly evaporate. Every single soul will know with terrifying, absolute clarity exactly what spiritual baggage they have brought to the scales of justice.

Ibn Ata'illah states: "The greatest tragedy of the worldly life is that man spends his days packing his bags for a journey, only to realize at the destination that he packed entirely the wrong currency." When the sky is stripped away, the only currency that retains any purchasing power is a pure heart and sincere deeds.

Verse 15
فَلَآ أُقْسِمُ بِٱلْخُنَّسِ
"So I swear by the retreating stars -"
Plain Understanding
God transitions from describing the future destruction of the cosmos to swearing an oath by its current, active mechanics. He swears by "Al-Khunnas," the celestial bodies (planets and stars) that recede, shrink back, or become invisible to the human eye during the day due to the overpowering light of the sun.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Imam Al-Qurtubi notes the profound parallel between the stars and the Resurrection. The stars do not cease to exist when the sun rises; they merely retreat from human vision, only to burst forth again when the darkness falls. Similarly, the human body does not permanently cease to exist when it is buried in the darkness of the grave; it is merely hidden from sight, waiting for the divine command to burst forth again on the Day of Judgment.

Verse 18
وَٱلصُّبْحِ إِذَا تَنَفَّسَ
"And by the dawn when it breathes"
Plain Understanding
God provides one of the most beautiful and poetic physical descriptions in the Qur'an. The dawn is personified as a living entity that "breathes" (Tanaffas). As the first rays of morning light pierce the darkness, the cold, stagnant air of the night gives way to the warm, active, expanding breeze of the morning, bringing biological life back to the earth.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali extracts the spiritual metaphor embedded in this sequence. The society of Mecca (and humanity before the revelation) was trapped in the heavy, suffocating darkness of ignorance ("the night as it closes in"). The descent of the Qur'an was the "breathing dawn"—a pure, life-giving light that broke the darkness of idolatry, allowing the suffocating human soul to finally inhale the fresh air of Tawhid (Monotheism).

Verse 19
إِنَّهُۥ لَقَوْلُ رَسُولٍۢ كَرِيمٍۢ
"Indeed, the Qur'an is a word [conveyed by] a noble messenger"
Plain Understanding
The subject of the four cosmic oaths is established. God swears by the stars, the night, and the dawn to confirm the absolute authenticity of the Qur'an. It is transmitted by a "Rasul Kareem" (a Noble Messenger). In the context of these specific verses, this refers to the Archangel Gabriel (Jibril), the divine courier entrusted with carrying the text from the heavens to Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

Verse 22
وَمَا صَاحِبُكُم بِمَجْنُونٍۢ
"And your companion is not [at all] mad."
Plain Understanding
Having established the flawless purity of the angelic transmitter (Gabriel), God now fiercely defends the human receiver. God explicitly rejects the Quraysh's smear campaign, declaring that the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ is absolutely free of madness ("Majnun").
Historical Context
God purposefully uses the term "Sahibukum" (your companion). The elite of Mecca were accusing the Prophet ﷺ of being a lunatic possessed by jinn. By calling him "your companion," God logically corners them: "You have lived with this man, traded with this man, and observed his flawless intellect and honesty for forty years. You know he is the most rational man in Mecca. Calling him crazy now simply because you dislike his message is a deliberate, obvious lie."

Verse 23
وَلَقَدْ رَءَاهُ بِٱلْأُفُقِ ٱلْمُبِينِ
"And he has already seen Gabriel in the clear horizon."
Plain Understanding
God validates the Prophet's ﷺ profound spiritual experience. The Prophet's claim of receiving revelation is not a hallucination or a dream. He physically, objectively saw the Archangel Gabriel in his true, overwhelming angelic form, dominating the clear, visible horizon.
Historical Context
According to authentic narrations (Sahih Al-Bukhari), the Prophet ﷺ only saw Gabriel in his true, unshielded angelic form twice in his life. The first time (referenced here) occurred in the valley of Mecca. The Prophet looked up and saw Gabriel sitting on a throne between the sky and the earth, possessing six hundred massive wings that completely blocked out the entire horizon. The sheer awe of this physical encounter proved he was dealing with the Sovereign of the heavens, not a localized jinn.

"So where are you going?"
Plain Understanding
After establishing the undeniable destruction of the universe, the flawless purity of Gabriel, the sanity of the Prophet, and the divine origin of the text, God poses a single, paralyzing, existential question to humanity: With all of these escapes blocked, where exactly are you running to?

Verse 28
لِمَن شَآءَ مِنكُمْ أَن يَسْتَقِيمَ
"For whoever wills among you to take a right course."
Plain Understanding
The application of this reminder is directly tied to human agency. The text is available to the entire world, but it will only benefit the individual who actively exercises their free will ("Sha'a") to pursue "Istiqamah" (to walk an upright, disciplined, straight path free from the deviations of the ego).
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali defines *Istiqamah*. It is the hardest spiritual station to maintain. It is easy to experience a temporary burst of faith and perform a massive deed. *Istiqamah* is the quiet, unbroken, daily discipline of maintaining the straight path when the initial emotional high has faded and the ego screams for deviation.

Verse 29 ۩
وَمَا تَشَآءُونَ إِلَّآ أَن يَشَآءَ ٱللَّهُ رَبُّ ٱلْعَـٰلَمِينَ
"And you do not will except that Allah wills - Lord of the worlds."
Plain Understanding
The Surah concludes by placing human free will within its proper, ultimate theological framework. While human beings must actively choose to walk the straight path (Verse 28), they must simultaneously recognize that their will is not entirely independent. No human action, choice, or movement can occur unless it is ultimately permitted, encompassed, and facilitated by the Sovereign Will of Allah, the Master of the Universe.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Al-Qurtubi notes how Verses 28 and 29 perfectly balance the Islamic theology of Predestination (Qadar). Verse 28 destroys the argument of the determinist (Jabriyyah) who claims humans are forced robots with no choice. Verse 29 destroys the argument of the extreme free-will advocate (Qadariyyah) who claims humans operate completely outside of God's control. You have a choice, but your choice operates strictly within the theater that God built and sustains.
