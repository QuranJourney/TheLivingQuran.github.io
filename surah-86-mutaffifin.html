Surah Al-Mutaffifin

The Defrauders • Makkah • 36 Verses
Surah Al-Mutaffifin establishes a terrifying psychological link between everyday economic corruption and the denial of the Hereafter. Revealed during a transitional period where ethical decay dominated the markets, Allah exposes the sheer delusion of those who ruthlessly extract their own rights while subtly shortchanging others. It serves as a stark, uncompromising declaration that absolute cosmic justice governs even the smallest worldly transaction.
Verse 1
وَيْلٌ لِّلْمُطَفِّفِينَ
"Woe to the defrauders."
Plain Understanding
A severe warning of destruction is declared against those who cheat others, even in the smallest, most seemingly insignificant amounts, when doing business.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
Ibn Abbas reported that when the Prophet (ﷺ) arrived in Madinah, the people there were notorious for cheating in weights and measures. Allah revealed these verses, and subsequently, they became the most precise in their measuring, terrified by the Divine threat.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Al-Ghazali notes that the "defrauder" is not merely the merchant. It represents anyone who demands their full rights from others—whether in relationships, respect, or wealth—while falling short in fulfilling their own duties towards Allah and creation.
WarningSocial Ethics
Verse 2
الَّذِينَ إِذَا اكْتَالُوا عَلَى النَّاسِ يَسْتَوْفُونَ
"Those who, when they take a measure from people, take in full."
Plain Understanding
When these corrupt individuals buy something or demand their own rights from others, they make absolutely sure they get every single bit of what is owed to them, leaving nothing behind and accepting no loss.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata'illah)
The human ego naturally leans toward self-preservation and greed. Ibn Ata'illah points out that seeking one's own rights obsessively while neglecting the rights of others is a symptom of profound spiritual blindness, severing the heart from the light of Divine justice.

Verse 3
وَإِذَا كَالُوهُمْ أَو وَّزَنُوهُمْ يُخْسِرُونَ
"But if they give by measure or by weight to them, they cause loss."
Plain Understanding
Conversely, when it is their turn to sell or give others their due, they intentionally give less than what is fair, subtly cheating the other person out of their rightful share to increase their own profit.
Purification of the Self (Al-Gilani)
True commerce for the believer is with Allah. Al-Gilani teaches that whoever diminishes the rights of people out of love for the worldly life has fundamentally bankrupted their soul in the Divine exchange, trading eternal proximity for temporary dust.

Verse 4
أَلَا يَظُنُّ أُولَٰئِكَ أَنَّهُم مَّبْعُوثُونَ
"Do they not think that they will be resurrected?"
Plain Understanding
The root cause of their corrupt behavior is exposed: they do not truly believe, or act as if they believe, that they will be brought back to life to answer to Allah for their actions.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
Scholars highlight that the verb yazunnu (think/have certainty) here serves as an astonishing psychological diagnosis. Even a mere suspicion or passing thought of facing Allah should be enough to stop a person from cheating; their actions prove they lack even this basic, minimal level of reflection.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Al-Ghazali explains that certainty (Yaqeen) in the Hereafter is the true anchor of morality. Without it, human ethics are entirely situational. The absence of this certainty allows the ego to easily justify exploitation.

Verse 6

"The Day when mankind will stand before the Lord of the worlds?"
Plain Understanding
On this final Day, all of humanity will stand perfectly still, waiting for judgment, completely stripped of their worldly power, connections, and wealth, facing the ultimate Creator and Master of all existence.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
It is authentically recorded that Abdullah ibn Umar once recited this Surah, and when he reached "the Lord of the worlds," he began to weep so intensely that he fell to his knees, utterly overwhelmed, and could not continue the recitation.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Standing before the Lord of the worlds in the Hereafter mirrors how one stands before Him in prayer today. Al-Ghazali advises that whoever straightens their posture and humbles their heart in daily prayer will find ease on the Day all of mankind stands in terror.

Verse 7

"No! Indeed, the record of the wicked is in Sijjeen."
Plain Understanding
A forceful rejection of the defrauders' false assumptions that they will escape accountability. Allah decrees that the permanent, inescapable record of these corrupt sinners is locked away in a suffocating, degraded realm known as Sijjeen.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
Early exegetes explain that "Sijjeen" derives from the root sijn (prison). It is a narrow, confined, and deeply restrictive realm located in the lowest earth, contrasting sharply with the vast, elevated expanse of the heavens where the records of the righteous are kept.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Sin actively restricts the soul. Al-Ghazali notes that just as Sijjeen is a physical prison in the Hereafter, the wicked have already imprisoned their own hearts in the worldly life through the heavy chains of greed, utterly blinding themselves to the expansive light of Allah's presence.

Verse 14

"No! Rather, the stain has covered their hearts of that which they were earning."
Plain Understanding
The constant, deliberate accumulation of sins creates a dark, impenetrable barrier over the human heart. This spiritual rust completely blocks out the light of truth, blinding these individuals to the horrific reality of their corrupt actions.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
Al-Qurtubi anchors this verse in an authentic hadith of the Prophet (ﷺ), who explained the physiological and spiritual mechanics of sin: "When a believer sins, a black spot appears on his heart. If he repents, asks for forgiveness, and gives up the sin, his heart is polished. But if he increases in sin, the spot increases until it covers his entire heart. That is the 'Raan' (stain) which Allah mentioned."
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Sins are not merely external actions; they are toxic psychological substances. Al-Ghazali emphasizes that repeated transgression fundamentally alters the spiritual organ. The heart hardens until it loses its innate capacity to recognize goodness, turning the person into a captive of their own unchecked ego.

Verse 15

"No! Indeed, from their Lord, that Day, they will be partitioned."
Plain Understanding
On the Day of Judgment, these arrogant sinners will suffer the ultimate, most devastating deprivation. They will be entirely blocked and physically veiled from seeing or experiencing the presence of their Creator.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
Classical scholars, including Imam Malik and Al-Shafi'i, powerfully deduced a profound theological doctrine from this verse. They reasoned that if the righteous believers were not going to see Allah in Paradise (the supreme reward), Allah would not have specifically threatened the disbelievers with the agonizing punishment of being "partitioned" or veiled from His Divine presence.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata'illah)
The physical veil in the Hereafter is the direct, terrifying manifestation of the spiritual veil they actively wove in the worldly life. Ibn Ata'illah explains that because they consciously chose to blind themselves to Allah's signs while on earth, Allah makes their blindness a permanent, inescapable reality in the next life.

Verse 34
فَالْيَوْمَ الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا مِنَ الْكُفَّارِ يَضْحَكُونَ
"So Today those who believed are laughing at the disbelievers,"
Plain Understanding
Absolute, flawless justice is finally executed on the Day of Judgment. The power dynamic is permanently reversed. The believers, now entirely secure and honored, look upon the shattered, humiliated reality of the arrogant disbelievers and find profound validation in their ruin.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
Scholars note the majestic, terrifying symmetry of Divine justice perfectly completing the narrative of the Surah. The temporary, hollow laughter of the Quraysh echoing in the dusty streets of Makkah is directly and permanently replaced by the eternal, vindicated laughter of the believers resting securely on the thrones of Paradise.

Verse 35
عَلَى الْأَرَائِكِ يَنظُرُونَ
"On adorned couches, observing."
Plain Understanding
Reclining in absolute majesty and untouchable peace, the believers calmly observe the horrific torment of those who once ruthlessly tortured and mocked them, witnessing the flawless, undeniable fulfillment of Allah's promises.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
The highest joy is witnessing the ultimate victory of truth over falsehood. Al-Ghazali emphasizes that their observation from the thrones is not rooted in petty, worldly vengeance, but in the overwhelming, awe-inspiring realization of Allah's perfect justice and the complete evaporation of human arrogance.

Verse 36
هَلْ ثُوِّبَ الْكُفَّارُ مَا كَانُوا يَفْعَلُونَ
"Have the disbelievers been rewarded for what they used to do?"
Plain Understanding
A final, devastatingly sarcastic question concludes the Surah. It confirms beyond any doubt that the disbelievers are not being wronged; they are simply receiving the exact, meticulously calculated "reward" for their lifetime of corruption, arrogance, and psychological warfare against the truth.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
The term thuwwiba (rewarded/recompensed) is deliberately deployed here as a form of intense psychological closure (Tahakkum). It seals the fate of the economic defrauders mentioned in the very first verse, proving conclusively that every single rigged scale, stolen right, and arrogant laugh has been perfectly, inescapably repaid.
