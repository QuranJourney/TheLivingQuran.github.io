Surah 'Abasa

He Frowned • Makkah • 42 Verses
The Divine Reprimand. Revealed when the Prophet ﷺ frowned at a blind man while preaching to the elite, delivering a gentle but firm divine correction to the Prophet ﷺ. It establishes a radical restructuring of human value: sincerity over status, ending with the terrifying reality of the Day of Judgment where all worldly ties shatter.
Verse 1
عَبَسَ وَتَوَلَّىٰٓ
"He frowned and turned away,"
Plain Understanding
The Surah opens abruptly with a description of a physical reaction. The Prophet ﷺ frowned and turned his face away in frustration during an interaction.
Historical Context
The Prophet ﷺ was deeply engaged in a crucial, high-stakes conversation with the billionaire elite of the Quraysh (including Abu Jahl, Umayyah ibn Khalaf, and Utbah). He was desperately trying to convert them, knowing that if the leadership accepted Islam, the brutal persecution of the Muslims would end. Suddenly, a poor, blind companion named Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum approached. Unable to see the elite company, he persistently interrupted the Prophet, begging to be taught verses of the Qur'an. Frustrated that this crucial diplomatic opportunity was being derailed, the Prophet ﷺ frowned and turned away from the blind man.
Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Imam Al-Qurtubi points out the breathtaking linguistic gentleness of God in this verse. Instead of directly confronting the Prophet with "You frowned," God uses the third-person pronoun "He frowned." This subtle grammatical shift softens the initial rebuke, protecting the immense honor of His beloved Messenger while still establishing the objective truth of the event.

Verse 2
أَن جَآءَهُ ٱلْأَعْمَىٰ
"Because there came to him the blind man."
Plain Understanding
God explicitly states the reason for the frown. The interruption came from a blind man who lacked the physical sight to realize he was breaching the protocols of an elite gathering.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
Imam al-Ghazali notes the profound spiritual contrast. The poor man's physical blindness was his legitimate excuse for interrupting, but it was his inner spiritual sight (his desperate sincerity for the truth) that made him beloved to God. The Makkan elite had perfect 20/20 physical vision, but were entirely, permanently blind in their hearts.

Verse 3
وَمَا يُدْرِيكَ لَعَلَّهُۥ يَزَّكَّىٰ
"But what would make you perceive, [O Muhammad], that perhaps he might be purified,"
Plain Understanding
The grammatical structure shifts from the third person to the direct second person ("you"), addressing the Prophet ﷺ directly. God questions the Prophet's strategic calculation: How could you know the internal state of this blind man? Perhaps his desperate seeking would have resulted in an immediate, profound spiritual purification ("Yazzakka").
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
Ibn Ata'illah extracts the ultimate metric of human value: "True worth is never found in political influence or wealth, but strictly in the heart's readiness to be purified. A single drop of sincerity from a beggar outweighs oceans of arrogance from a king." God corrected the Prophet's strategic prioritization to align with the Divine priority: sincerity over status.

Verse 4

"Or that he might receive admonition, and the admonition would benefit him?"
Plain Understanding
God continues to emphasize the missed opportunity. If the Prophet had turned his attention to the blind man, the man would have internalized the divine reminder (Thikra), and that reminder would have yielded immediate, practical, and eternal benefits for his soul.
Purification of the Self (Al-Gilani)
Sheikh Abdul Qadir al-Gilani reflects on the duty of the spiritual guide (Murshid). The purpose of revelation is transformation, not mere intellectual transmission. The poor man came seeking medicine for a bleeding soul, while the rich men were only interested in debating the ingredients. The true physician must always attend to the dying patient, not the arrogant spectator.

Plain Understanding

Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Al-Qurtubi identifies *Istighna* (delusional self-sufficiency) as the ultimate, terminal spiritual disease. When a human being feels they already possess everything they need to survive and thrive, they personally lock the door to heaven from the inside. God's mercy only flows into a vessel that recognizes it is empty.

Verse 10

"From him you are distracted."
Plain Understanding
The divine correction is finalized. The Prophet ﷺ is gently reprimanded for allowing his grand political strategy (converting the powerful) to distract him ("Talahha") from the pure, trembling heart of the individual poor seeker.
Historical Context
The Prophet ﷺ absorbed this divine correction with absolute, beautiful humility. He never hid this Surah, even though it corrected him publicly. For the rest of his life, whenever Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum entered the room, the Prophet ﷺ would literally spread his own cloak on the ground for the blind man to sit on, smiling and saying: "Welcome to the one for whom my Lord reprimanded me!" The Prophet later honored him by leaving him in charge of the entire city of Medina on multiple occasions when the army marched out.

Verse 17

Plain Understanding
God issues a severe, terrifying reprimand directed at the arrogance of the human ego. The phrase "Qutila" literally translates to "May he be killed" or "Cursed is he." Divine Wisdom (Al-Qurtubi)
Imam Al-Qurtubi explains that this curse is specifically aimed at the wealthy, arrogant elite of Mecca (and by extension, all tyrannical egos throughout time) who felt they were too important to sit with a blind, poor man or submit to the revelation. Their extreme ingratitude triggers the absolute wrath of the Sustainer.
